Story of Ameera

War didn’t stop my period

Lived Experience

The war

This is Ameera.
War forced her from home
into her uncle’s house,
in another state in Sudan.

Displaced, Not Settled

She is safe — but not at ease.
Too many changes.
Too many people.
No privacy.

Hours

When her period comes,
the bathroom is not always hers.
Guests. Voices. Movement.
She cannot change her pad.
So she waits — for hours.

Silence!

She has cramps.
No painkillers are available where she lives.
They say:
“You’ll get over it when you marry.”
“Don’t show pain — men are around.”
So she learns silence.

Water

This bathroom is not hers.
Sometimes, there is no water
for days.
She cannot clean herself.
She cries.

Night is the Only Witness

She cannot wash or throw anything away.
The house is always full.
So she wakes before dawn
or stays awake at night,
cleaning in pain.

Money

The pads are poor quality.
The market is far.
No car. No money.
Food comes first.
Her period —
last on the list.

For Too Long

She wears one pad
far longer than she should.
Not from ignorance —
but from lack.

Improvise!

When the pads run out,
she thinks:
“Old clothes.”
“Cotton. Tissues.”
“It’s the last days…
maybe I’ll just endure.”

Freedom

Her period becomes a source of fear.
She resents her femininity.
She prays it won’t come. She wants to be free.

The Cost

Infections. UTIs.
Irregular cycles.
Her health declines —
quietly.

Not Just Ameera

This is not Ameera’s story alone.
There are thousands of Ameeras —
displaced,
unseen,
unable to manage their cycles.
